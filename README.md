# ai-social-good-project-FundMIS
BUS4-110A Project 3: AI for Social Good Ideathon
##
Who specifically is affected, and how? Name one user — not a broad group, but a specific person in a specific situation. The more precisely you can describe who is underserved and why, the more credible your proposal will be.
  Guadalupe, a 67 year old Spanish-speaking resident in Tulare County, California is directly affected by this problem. When she notices brown water coming from her tap, she wants to report it but encounters multiple barriers, the official reporting portal is only available in English, required a parcel number (APN) she does not know, and times out quickly. With no clear alternative, she gives up and continues using the water despite potential contamination. Her situation illustrates how an existing systems fails a specific individual due to language, technical requirements, and poor usability, leaving her underserved and at risk. 

What is the exact failure point? Is the problem that information does not exist, exists but cannot be found, can be found but not understood, or is understood but has no clear path to action? Identify one failure and explain why it matters.
  The core failure point is that the system exists but cannot be effectively acted upon. Information about how to report water contamination is technically available, and a reporting pathway exists, but it is inaccessible to users like Guadalupe. This is not a lack of information but a breakdown in usability and access at the final step between recognizing a problem and taking action. This matters because it prevents timely reporting of contamination, limits the data available to authorities, and forces to make unsafe choices. 

Which AI capability addresses that failure? Match the capability to the problem: text generation for drafting or explaining, structured extraction for turning messy input into usable data, image recognition for visual inspection or reporting. Justify the match from your lab experience.
  The AI capability the best addresses the failure is a combination of image recognition and structured data extraction. Instead of requiring users to navigate complez forms, the system allows them to submit a photo and a short description in any language. Image recognition identifies visible signs of contamination such as dicoloration or sediment, while structured extraction converts the user's input into standardized, actionable data like contaminant type, urgency level, and location. This directly aligns with the problem by replacing a high-friction reporting process with a simple, intuitive input method and transforming it into usable output for authorities. 

What is the workflow? Describe what goes in, what the AI does, what comes out, and who acts on the output. A tool that produces useful output that no one reads is not a solution.
  The worflow begins with the user taking a photo of their tap water and writing a brief complaint in their preferred language, with location automatically captured via GPS. The AI system analyzes the image to detect potential contamination and processes the text to extract key details. It then generates a structured report containing relevant fields such as location, risk score, and contaminant category. This report is sent to the appopriate water authority, where a human reviewer evaluates high-risk cases before action is taken. The user receives a confirmation message in their language, ensuring they are informed and included in the process. 

What is the main ethical risk? Name a specific harm that could result from an incorrect AI output. Identify who controls the system and whether the AI is supporting or replacing a human decision.
  The main ethical risk is that AI could produce an incorrect assessment, such as misclassifying harmless rust as a serious bacterial contamination. This could cause unnecessary panic, lead individuals to avoid safe water, or divert limited regulatory resources. The system is controlled by a public agency, and the AI is designed to support and not replace human decision-making. To mitigate harm, human review is required before alerts are finalized, especially for high-risk cases. While this introduces a delay, it balances the need for timely responses with the importance of accuracy and trust.
